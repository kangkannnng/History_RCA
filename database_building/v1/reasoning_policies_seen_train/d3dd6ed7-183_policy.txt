[Trigger]
- Trace anomaly pattern: Frontend service shows increased response time for ProductCatalogService calls, with latency rising from approximately 11.4 seconds to 13.8 seconds.
- Metric anomaly pattern: Multiple pods across different nodes exhibit process counts, CPU usage, and network packet transmission metrics dropping to zero simultaneously.
- Metric anomaly pattern: No error logs are generated during the anomaly window, indicating a silent failure characteristic.

[Focus Evidence]
Primary evidence (core causal indicators):
- Evidence 1: The `pod_processes` metric for pods `productcatalogservice-0` (node aiops-k8s-06), `redis-cart-0` (node aiops-k8s-08), and `frontend-2` (node aiops-k8s-04) all drop from a normal value to zero at the same time. This matters because it shows an abrupt and synchronized termination of application processes across multiple, functionally independent services on different compute nodes, pointing to an external, cluster-wide trigger rather than an internal application fault.
- Evidence 2: Network activity metrics (`pod_network_transmit_packets`, `pod_network_receive_packets`) for numerous pods across several services also drop to zero concurrently with the process termination. This matters because it indicates a complete cessation of inter-service communication, which is a systemic network-level symptom consistent with a broader infrastructure disruption affecting multiple nodes simultaneously.

Secondary evidence (downstream effects or noise):
- Evidence 3: Increased trace latency for the `frontend` and `productcatalogservice`. This is a consequence, not a cause, as the latency spike is the observable user-facing impact resulting from the underlying pod terminations and network interruption.
- Evidence 4: The absence of application error logs can be deprioritized in the causal chain because it is a characteristic symptom of infrastructure-level failures (like node reboots or network partitions) where applications are terminated abruptly without a chance to log errors, rather than a clue pointing to a specific application bug.

[Reasoning]
Step 1: Initial hypothesis based on primary evidence
"Given the synchronized termination of processes and cessation of network traffic across multiple pods on distinct nodes, we suspect a systemic failure at the cluster orchestration or infrastructure layer, as simultaneous, coordinated failure across independent components suggests a common external cause."

Step 2: Verification or elimination
"If this were caused by a cascading failure starting from a single faulty pod, we would expect to see a sequence of failures or time-lagged errors in dependent services, and likely some error logs from the initially failing application;
However, actual observation shows perfectly synchronized metric drops to zero across unrelated services and a complete absence of application error logs;
This rules out an application-level defect or a simple service dependency cascade as the primary cause."

Step 3: Convergence to conclusion
"The combination of synchronized pod lifecycle events across multiple nodes and the concomitant, system-wide network traffic halt indicates a disruption at the cluster control plane or node infrastructure level. The precise one-second timing window suggests a coordinated event, such as a control plane hiccup, node scheduling anomaly, or network partition, that caused multiple compute nodes or their managed pods to become unresponsive simultaneously."

[Conclusion]
"The root cause is a cluster-level infrastructure or control plane disruption that led to the synchronized termination and network isolation of application pods across multiple compute nodes, manifesting as increased service latency due to the loss of backend instances."
