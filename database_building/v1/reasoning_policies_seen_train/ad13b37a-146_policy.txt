- Trace anomaly pattern: Multiple calls from the frontend service to the recommendationservice failed with "connection refused" errors, and the frontend service subsequently returned HTTP 500 errors to users.
- Metric anomaly pattern: The maximum response time (rrt_max) for one instance of the recommendationservice increased by over 51%.

[Focus Evidence]
Primary evidence (core causal indicators):
- Evidence 1: The pod_processes count for every recommendationservice replica increased simultaneously. This metric directly indicates an instability in the service's runtime process lifecycle, suggesting an interruption or instability event.
- Evidence 2: Trace data shows explicit "connection refused" errors when other services attempt to communicate with recommendationservice. This is a direct signal of service unavailability at the network level, which aligns with a process being unreachable.

Secondary evidence (downstream effects or noise):
- Evidence 3: Multiple cluster nodes showed a decrease in available memory. This is a consequential effect, as process restarts can free and re-allocate memory, but it is observed across several nodes and not isolated to the one hosting the primary affected service, making it a broader symptom rather than the specific cause.
- Evidence 4: Downstream services like cartservice and checkoutservice experienced increased latency or altered performance patterns. These are propagation effects caused by the upstream service becoming unavailable, which can trigger retries, timeouts, or circuit-breaking behavior in dependents.

[Reasoning]
Step 1: Initial hypothesis based on primary evidence
"Given the simultaneous increase in pod_processes across all replicas of a single service, coupled with immediate connection refusal errors from its network endpoint, we suspect a widespread instability or restart event affecting the core runtime processes of that service."

Step 2: Verification or elimination
"If this service unavailability were caused by a generalized resource shortage on its host node, we would expect to see correlated severe performance degradation or failures in other co-located services on the same node; However, actual observation shows connection errors are highly specific to this one service, and trace anomalies for other services on the same nodes are varied and less severe; This rules out a primary node-level resource contention as the root cause and focuses the issue on the service's own process lifecycle."

Step 3: Convergence to conclusion
"The combination of direct process count anomalies and immediate, persistent network-level unavailability indicates a cascading failure originating from the service's inability to maintain stable process execution. The restart cycle creates a temporary window of unavailability, causing upstream callers to fail."

[Conclusion]
"The root cause is a recurring instability in the service's process execution, leading to periodic termination and restart. This lifecycle disruption creates temporary but complete unavailability, causing all incoming requests to fail during the restart windows and triggering error propagation through the system."
