[Trigger]
- Log anomaly pattern: Repeated log entries showing execution of a specific stress test rule across all instances of a service, with associated thread creation and agent initialization errors
- Metric anomaly pattern: Sudden and extreme increase in computational resource consumption for all instances of a single service, coinciding with significant degradation in response time metrics for that same service
- Trace anomaly pattern: Severe latency increase in requests targeting the affected service, propagating delays to upstream calling services

[Focus Evidence]
Primary evidence (core causal indicators):
- Evidence 1: Logs show a specific, named stress-testing rule being actively executed hundreds of times across all replicas of the service during the anomaly window - this directly points to an active, targeted computational workload injection.
- Evidence 2: Resource consumption metrics show a near-zero to high utilization spike isolated to the pods of the service mentioned in the logs, perfectly correlated in time with the log activity - this confirms the injected workload is consuming resources.

Secondary evidence (downstream effects or noise):
- Evidence 3: Trace data shows markedly increased latency for calls to the affected service - this is a predictable consequence of its pods being saturated by the injected computational load.
- Evidence 4: Minor metric fluctuations in other services and node-level resources - these are secondary effects or unrelated noise, as the primary, extreme anomaly is concentrated and explained by the first two evidences.

[Reasoning]
Step 1: Initial hypothesis based on primary evidence
"Given the repeated log entries documenting the execution of a stress-test rule and the concurrent, drastic spike in resource usage for the same service, we suspect an externally induced computational overload targeted specifically at that service component."

Step 2: Verification or elimination
"If this were caused by an inherent bug or misconfiguration within the service, we would expect to see error patterns related to application logic or resource leaks, not logs explicitly describing a test rule execution. The actual observation shows logs that are diagnostic of a testing framework being activated, which rules out an intrinsic service failure as the primary cause."

Step 3: Convergence to conclusion
"The combination of the diagnostic logs identifying a stress-test agent and the correlated resource exhaustion metrics indicates the root cause is a deliberate, external injection of a heavy computational workload into the service, designed to simulate failure conditions."

[Conclusion]
"The root cause is the targeted activation of a stress-testing mechanism on the service, which deliberately consumed its computational resources to near saturation, thereby inducing severe performance degradation for all its instances."
