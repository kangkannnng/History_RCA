[Trigger]
- Log anomaly pattern: No error logs were detected during the fault period, which is inconsistent with other error indicators, suggesting potential logging gaps or intermittent fault behavior.
- Metric anomaly pattern: Client error ratio and overall error ratio spiked from baseline near zero to elevated levels (6.1-7.95), accompanied by a significant increase in timeout occurrences (thousands of instances) across frontend service instances.
- Trace anomaly pattern: High frequency of errors in the frontend service where requests failed to locate intended resources, with over 5000 such occurrences, and subsequent increased latency in downstream services like cartservice and shippingservice.

[Focus Evidence]
Primary evidence (core causal indicators):
- Evidence 1: Trace data reveals a concentrated pattern of errors in the frontend service, with thousands of failures indicating that incoming requests could not be properly processed or routed. This matters because it identifies the entry point where issues originate, suggesting a problem in request handling.
- Evidence 2: Metric data shows simultaneous spikes in error ratios and timeouts exclusively in frontend instances, with no corresponding resource usage anomalies (e.g., normal CPU usage). This supports that the issue is related to request processing logic rather than resource constraints.

Secondary evidence (downstream effects or noise):
- Evidence 3: Downstream services such as cartservice and shippingservice exhibit increased response times, with latency multipliers up to 4.15 times normal. This is a consequence likely due to retries or cascading load from frontend errors, not a primary cause.
- Evidence 4: Log data absence of errors, while anomalous, does not contradict other evidence and may indicate logging system limitations or that errors occurred at a level not captured by application logs, allowing it to be deprioritized in causal analysis.

[Reasoning]
Step 1: Initial hypothesis based on primary evidence
"Given the high concentration of errors and timeouts in the frontend service, with no resource bottlenecks observed, we suspect a flaw in the frontend's request processing logic that causes widespread client-side failures."

Step 2: Verification or elimination
"If this were caused by infrastructure issues like network delays or pod failures, we would expect to see correlated anomalies in resource metrics or errors distributed across multiple services. However, actual observation shows error spikes isolated to frontend instances and propagated latency effects only in dependent services, with resource usage remaining stable. This rules out infrastructure-level causes and points to an application-level issue in the frontend."

Step 3: Convergence to conclusion
"The combination of error concentration in traces and metric spikes in frontend instances, along with the absence of resource constraints, indicates that the root cause is a misconfiguration or logic error in the frontend service. This leads to incorrect request handling, resulting in client errors and timeouts that cascade to downstream services due to increased load or retry mechanisms."

[Conclusion]
"The root cause is a failure in the frontend service where a significant portion of incoming requests are mishandled due to incorrect configuration or processing logic, causing persistent client errors and timeouts that propagate delays to dependent services."
