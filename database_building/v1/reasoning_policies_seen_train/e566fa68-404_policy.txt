[Trigger]
- Log anomaly pattern: Error logs consistently show connection failures with messages indicating inability to resolve a service name during connection attempts.
- Metric anomaly pattern: Significant spikes in error ratios and server error ratios for a dependent service, increasing from near zero to high percentages.
- Trace anomaly pattern: Traces reveal that inter-service calls experience extreme latency increases, specifically when attempting to connect to another service, correlating with the resolution errors.

[Focus Evidence]
Primary evidence (core causal indicators):
- Evidence 1: Log entries with specific error messages about failure to resolve a service name on a resolution server – this directly indicates a breakdown in the service discovery mechanism preventing connections.
- Evidence 2: Trace data showing that latency for specific service calls spikes dramatically only when resolution errors occur – this links the resolution issue to performance degradation and confirms the causal relationship.

Secondary evidence (downstream effects or noise):
- Evidence 3: Metric spikes in error ratios for the calling service – this is a consequence of the connection failures resulting from the resolution problem.
- Evidence 4: Minor increases in error ratios for upstream services – this can be deprioritized as it is a ripple effect from the primary failure point and does not indicate the root cause.

[Reasoning]
Step 1: Initial hypothesis based on primary evidence
Given the repeated log errors showing inability to resolve a critical service name, we suspect a failure in the name resolution infrastructure.

Step 2: Verification or elimination
If this were caused by the target service having internal availability issues, we would expect error patterns such as timeouts or service-specific failures after connection establishment; however, actual observation shows errors specifically during the connection initiation phase due to resolution failure, ruling out internal service problems.

Step 3: Convergence to conclusion
The combination of resolution errors in logs and the correlated latency spikes in traces indicates that the root cause is a disruption in the service discovery process, where essential dependencies cannot be resolved, leading to cascading failures.

[Conclusion]
The root cause is a failure in the name resolution mechanism that prevents a service from being discovered, causing connection attempts to fail and resulting in increased error rates and latency in dependent services.
