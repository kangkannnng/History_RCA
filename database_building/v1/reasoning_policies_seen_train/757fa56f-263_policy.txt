[Trigger]
- Metric anomaly pattern: Pod process count increased from 1.0 to 12.0 across all instances of a specific service, accompanied by a sharp rise in CPU utilization.
- Trace anomaly pattern: Response times for calls to this service escalated dramatically, with latency multiplying up to 68 times the normal baseline.

[Focus Evidence]
Primary evidence (core causal indicators):
- Evidence 1: Pod process count surge from 1.0 to 12.0 - this indicates an abnormal multiplication of processes within the pod, suggesting internal process lifecycle disruptions or resource leaks that directly impact performance.
- Evidence 2: CPU usage spike coinciding with process count increase - demonstrates that the additional processes are consuming computational resources, leading to saturation and reduced capacity for request handling.

Secondary evidence (downstream effects or noise):
- Evidence 3: Elevated response times in upstream services - this is a consequential effect, as delays propagate through the dependency chain due to the primary service's degradation.
- Evidence 4: Minor latency variations in other services - these are less pronounced and likely secondary effects, not indicative of the core issue.

[Reasoning]
Step 1: Initial hypothesis based on primary evidence
"Given the concurrent rise in pod process count and CPU usage for the affected service, we suspect an internal process management anomaly causing resource contention and performance degradation."

Step 2: Verification or elimination
"If this were caused by external factors such as increased load or network congestion, we would expect broader latency patterns across multiple services or different resource metric behaviors; however, actual observation shows isolated process count anomalies and CPU saturation specifically in this service's pods, with tightly correlated latency spikes in dependent calls. This rules out widespread infrastructure issues and narrows the focus to application-level process behavior."

Step 3: Convergence to conclusion
"The combination of process count multiplication and CPU usage surge confirms that the pod is experiencing abnormal process spawning or lifecycle events, leading to resource exhaustion and significantly impaired request processing capabilities."

[Conclusion]
"The root cause is an internal process anomaly within the service pods, where an unexpected increase in process count resulted in CPU saturation and substantially increased response times, cascading to affect dependent services."
