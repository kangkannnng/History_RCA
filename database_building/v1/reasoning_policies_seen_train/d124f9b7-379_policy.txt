[Trigger]
- Trace anomaly pattern: Multiple service calls show severe latency increases during the anomaly period. Calls to productcatalogservice from checkoutservice increased from ~10 seconds to ~94 seconds, and from recommendationservice from ~3 seconds to ~39 seconds. Calls from frontend to recommendationservice also saw significant latency increases.
- Metric anomaly pattern: The pod_processes count for a specific instance surged from 1.0 to 9.0. Concurrently, its pod_cpu_usage spiked from 0.01 to 0.4. The maximum response time (rrt_max) for this instance increased dramatically from ~16 seconds to ~193 seconds.
- Log anomaly pattern: No explicit error logs were detected for the affected component during the anomaly window, indicating the issue manifested as performance degradation without generating failure logs.

[Focus Evidence]
Primary evidence (core causal indicators):
- Evidence 1: The pod_processes metric for the instance surged from 1.0 to 9.0 - This indicates an abnormal proliferation of processes within the container, which directly consumes system resources and can lead to resource contention.
- Evidence 2: The pod_cpu_usage for the same instance spiked from 0.01 to 0.4 - This shows a severe increase in computational resource consumption, aligning with the process surge and pointing to saturation of available CPU capacity.

Secondary evidence (downstream effects or noise):
- Evidence 3: Trace data shows severe latency increases for calls made to this specific instance from dependent services like checkoutservice and recommendationservice - This is a consequence of the instance's degraded processing capability, not the originating cause.
- Evidence 4: Other services like recommendationservice and frontend show increased response times in trace and metric data - These are secondary effects or cascading impacts due to their dependency on the primary affected instance.

[Reasoning]
Step 1: Initial hypothesis based on primary evidence
"Given the concurrent, severe surge in both the number of processes and CPU utilization for a single service instance, we suspect a resource saturation event caused by uncontrolled process creation within that container."

Step 2: Verification or elimination
"If this were caused by an external load surge or a downstream dependency failure, we would expect to see high CPU utilization across multiple instances or preceded by network/timeout errors in traces/logs; However, actual observation shows the process count and CPU spike are isolated to one instance, and the latency originates from calls *to* this instance, not from it; This rules out an external traffic surge or a downstream bottleneck as the primary cause."

Step 3: Convergence to conclusion
"The combination of the isolated process explosion and the consequent CPU saturation within the instance, coupled with the observed severe latency for all inbound calls to it, indicates that the root cause is internal to that instance. The abnormal process behavior directly consumed excessive computational resources, degrading its ability to handle requests and causing cascading delays for its callers."

[Conclusion]
"The root cause is an internal resource exhaustion event within a specific service instance, triggered by an abnormal and sustained increase in the number of active processes. This led to CPU saturation, severely impairing the instance's request processing capacity and resulting in high latency for all dependent services."
