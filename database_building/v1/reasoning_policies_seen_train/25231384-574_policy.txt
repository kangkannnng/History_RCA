Primary evidence (core causal indicators):
- Evidence 1: A sharp 152.83% rise in a key database internal queue metric - This indicates a fundamental processing backlog or scheduling constraint within the data storage layer itself, suggesting it is the origin point.
- Evidence 2: Exhaustion of available memory on a specific node, with usage rate surging from 30% to 64% - This shows a severe resource constraint on the infrastructure hosting the database component, which can directly throttle its operational performance.

Secondary evidence (downstream effects or noise):
- Evidence 3: Extreme latency degradation (6.9-18.4x) for queries to a service that depends on the database - This is a classic downstream symptom; the service's performance issue is a consequence of its dependency being impaired, not an independent failure.
- Evidence 4: Absence of error logs from the affected service - This rules out an intrinsic bug or crash within the service application logic, reinforcing that its problem is externally induced.

[Reasoning]
Step 1: Initial hypothesis based on primary evidence
"Given the simultaneous severe spike in the database's internal queue metric and the drastic exhaustion of memory on its host node, we suspect a resource saturation event is causing a critical bottleneck in the data storage layer's ability to process requests."

Step 2: Verification or elimination
"If this were caused by a primary defect in the front-end application service, we would expect to see error logs or metrics pointing to its internal failure modes;
However, actual observation shows no service errors, but does show catastrophic latency specifically for its database-dependent operations;
This rules out the application service as the root cause and supports it being a victim of a deeper dependency failure."

Step 3: Convergence to conclusion
"The combination of the direct resource exhaustion evidence on the database's node and the corresponding explosion in its internal processing queue indicates that node-level resource starvation directly induced a severe processing backlog in the storage layer. The downstream service latency is the direct and expected outcome of this backlog."

[Conclusion]
"The root cause is a node-level resource exhaustion event that created a critical bottleneck in the foundational data storage layer, manifesting as a severe processing queue backlog which then propagated as high latency to all dependent services."
