- Metric anomaly pattern: Error ratios for frontend services spiked from zero to over 30%, indicating a sudden increase in failed requests.
- Trace anomaly pattern: High frequency of failed calls to a downstream service, with trace data showing connection refused errors for specific operations.

[Focus Evidence]
Primary evidence (core causal indicators):
- Evidence 1: Log entries consistently report connection refused errors targeting the same endpoint (IP and port) from multiple frontend instances - this matters because it directly indicates that a specific downstream service is unreachable, suggesting a localized accessibility issue.
- Evidence 2: Trace data confirms that calls to the downstream service fail repeatedly with connection errors, and these errors are the direct cause of frontend request failures - this supports the log evidence by pinpointing the service where failures originate.

Secondary evidence (downstream effects or noise):
- Evidence 3: Error ratio metrics for frontend services show significant spikes - this is a consequence, as frontend errors are propagated from the downstream service's inaccessibility, not a root cause.
- Evidence 4: Increased latency in other services, such as Redis or cartservice - this can be deprioritized because it appears as secondary performance degradation, likely due to cascading effects or unrelated factors, given the primary connection issue.

[Reasoning]
Step 1: Initial hypothesis based on primary evidence
"Given the consistent connection refused errors in logs when frontend services attempt to communicate with a specific endpoint, we suspect that the downstream service has become inaccessible, possibly due to a configuration mismatch or service unavailability."

Step 2: Verification or elimination
"If this were caused by a widespread network failure, we would expect connectivity issues across multiple services and diverse endpoints. However, actual observation shows that errors are isolated to one specific IP and port, with other services functioning normally. This rules out a general network problem."
"If the issue stemmed from the frontend services themselves, we would anticipate internal processing errors or resource exhaustion. But the logs indicate outgoing connection failures, pointing to an external dependency as the source."

Step 3: Convergence to conclusion
"The combination of direct connection refusal evidence from logs and traces, along with the resulting error ratio spikes in frontend services, indicates that the root cause is the downstream service's failure to accept connections, likely due to a misconfiguration in service accessibility or deployment state."

[Conclusion]
"The root cause is the target service's inaccessibility leading to connection refusal, which cascades into request failures and error propagation in dependent services."
