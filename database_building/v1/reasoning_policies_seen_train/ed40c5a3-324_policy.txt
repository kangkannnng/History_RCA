[Trigger]
- Log anomaly pattern: No error logs were detected during the fault period, suggesting a silent performance issue without explicit error signals.
- Metric anomaly pattern: Response time metrics showed a severe increase, with one service experiencing over 190% degradation in median response time and maximum response time.
- Trace anomaly pattern: Multiple frontend instances exhibited significant delays when calling a specific downstream service, with delays 4.0 to 4.8 times higher than normal and high anomaly frequency.

[Focus Evidence]
Primary evidence (core causal indicators):
- Evidence 1: The most severe response time degradation was observed in a specific service instance, with median response time increasing from approximately 4.3 seconds to over 185 seconds and maximum response time rising from about 18 seconds to over 625 seconds - this matters because it represents the largest deviation from normal performance, indicating a critical issue at this point.
- Evidence 2: Trace data confirmed that all frontend instances had prolonged delays when invoking the same downstream service, with 22 occurrences of anomalies - this supports that the problem originates in this downstream service, as consistent delays across multiple callers point to a common dependency issue.

Secondary evidence (downstream effects or noise):
- Evidence 3: Frontend services also showed increased response times, with changes around 140-160%, but less severe than the downstream service - this is a consequence, as frontend services depend on the degraded service, leading to propagated delays.
- Evidence 4: Node memory usage changes and other service delays were present, such as a node memory increase of about 25% and Redis operation delays up to 9.2 times normal - these can be deprioritized because their impact is smaller and they may be secondary effects or unrelated noise.

[Reasoning]
Step 1: Initial hypothesis based on primary evidence
"Given the extreme response time increase in the downstream service and corresponding trace delays across all frontend callers, we suspect a performance degradation originating within that downstream service."

Step 2: Verification or elimination
"If this were caused by a node-wide resource constraint, we would expect similar severe degradation in other services on the same node; however, other services on the affected node show minimal or no comparable response time issues, ruling out a broad node resource problem."
"If the issue were in the frontend services themselves, we would observe errors or delays starting from frontend-initiated operations independently; however, trace patterns show delays specifically in calls to the downstream service, indicating the problem is isolated to that service's processing."

Step 3: Convergence to conclusion
"The combination of the most severe metric anomaly in the downstream service and trace evidence showing consistent delays from all upstream callers indicates that the root cause is internal to that service, causing a cascade of performance degradation upstream without generating error logs."

[Conclusion]
"The root cause is a severe internal performance degradation in the downstream service, leading to substantial delays in request processing that propagate to dependent frontend services, manifesting as a silent failure without error log generation."
